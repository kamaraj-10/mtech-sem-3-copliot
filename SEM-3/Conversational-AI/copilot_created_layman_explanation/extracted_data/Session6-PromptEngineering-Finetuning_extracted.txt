Prompt structure FrameworkEffective prompts follow a consistent structure for clarity and control:•Priming: This clearly defines the role of the LLM. (Assign a Persona)•Style and Tone: It sets expectations for clear, concise, and jargon-free language with a sought tone.•Error Handling: This guides the LLM to politely decline requests outside its defined scope.•Content (static or dynamic or both): Query, examples, data, etc.•Output Formatting: The format ensures the response can be easily used by an application or displayed in a structured way.The goal is to ensureClarity, Consistency, and Control. Without structure, LLMs can be unpredictable. With structure, they become reliable tools.

ToTExample: Game of 24Problem: Make 24 using {4, 9, 10, 13}Step 1: Decomposition (Generate Thought Candidates)• Thought A: 13 − 9 = 4 → Remaining: {4, 4, 10}• Thought B: 10 − 4 = 6 → Remaining: {6, 9, 13}• Thought C: 4 + 9 = 13 → Remaining: {13, 10, 13}Step 2: Self-Evaluation (Score Each Path)• A: 10+4+4=18 ✗| 4×4+10=26 ✗(Unlikely)• B: Remaining {6, 9, 13}—possible but difficult• C: {13, 10, 13}—hard to resolve (Likely bad)Step 3: Pruning & Search (Discard weak branches, expand promising ones)

ToT: Expanding the Best PathContinuing from Thought B: {6, 9, 13}The model expands the most promising branch further with new thought candidates:• B1: 13 − 9 = 4 → Remaining: {6, 4} → 6 ×4 = 24 ✓SUCCESS!• B2: 13 + 9 = 22 → Remaining: {6, 22} → Can't easily reach 24• B3: 9 − 6 = 3 → Remaining: {3, 13} → 13 ×3 = 39 (too high)Solution Found: (13 − 9) ×4 + 6 = 4 ×4 + 6 = 22... Meta-Prompting: The Model Writes Its Own PromptsAutomatic Prompt Engineer (APE) —introduced by Zhou et al. (2022), arXiv:2211.01910 —turns the LLM on itself. Instead of a human writing the best prompt for a target task, an 'APE LLM' is asked to generate and evaluate candidate prompts, which are then executed by a 'target LLM'. The best-performing prompt is selected automatically.The APE Pipeline1.Provide the APE LLM with demonstrations (input→outputexamples) of the target task.2.Instruct the APE LLM to generate N candidate prompt instructions (e.g., N=10–50).3.Execute each candidate prompt on a validation set using the target LLM.4.Score each prompt by accuracy (or another metric) on the validation set.5.Return the highest-scoring prompt for use in production.Step-1: Instruction GenerationA language model is given a task and a few examples (input–output pairs).It generates multiplecandidate prompts or instructions.Step-2: Prompt EvaluationThese prompts are tested by running them through atarget model.The outputs are scored based onperformance metrics(e.g., accuracy, relevance, coherence).Step-3: SelectionThebest-performing promptis selected for use.

Guidelines for the APE Meta-Prompt•Clearly define the APE's meta-role: 'You are an expert prompt engineer.'•Describe the target task in detail with 5–10 high-quality demonstrations.•Specify what makes an effective prompt: clarity, specificity, correct output format.•Ask for diverse candidates: 'Generate 10 different prompt instructions, each with a distinct angle.'•Encourage iteration: 'Refine your best candidate based on the following failure cases.'
Demonstrations → Automatic Prompt Engineer (APE) → Optimized PromptReference: Zhou, D., et al. (2022): Large Language Models Are Human-Level Prompt Engineers.arXivpreprint:arXiv:2211.01910SOTA (2024)APE-discovered prompts matched or outperformed human-written prompts on 24 of 24 NLP tasks evaluated by Zhou et al.

Language Modeling ≠ Following InstructionsPre-trained LLMs are optimized for next-token prediction, not for following user instructions or maintaining alignment with human intent.Problem Example:Prompt: "Explain the moon landing to a 6-year-old."Completion (unconstrained): "Explain gravity to a 6-year-old... Explain relativity... Explain the big bang..."The model continues the pattern rather than answering the question. It needs fine-tuning to follow instructions reliably.
Ouyang, L., et al. (2022). Training language models to follow instructions with human feedback. NeurIPS 2022.

Pretraining can improve NLP applications by serving as parameter initialization.
• Start with pretrained weights from foundational models• Fine-tune on smaller, task-specific datasets• Dramatically reduces data and compute requirementsResult: Better performance, faster training, lower costs

Self-Instruct: Aligning Language Models with Self-Generated InstructionsWang et al. 'Self-Instruct: Aligning Language Models with Self-Generated Instructions' (presented at ACL 2023) makes four primary contributions to the field:1.Problem identification: articulates the instruction-following gap in pre-trained LLMs and frames it as a solvable data generation problem.2.Pipeline design: proposes the four-step pipeline (generate → classify → instance generate → filter) that is now widely adopted in the field.3.Empirical validation: demonstrates near-InstructGPTperformance at a fraction of the cost on SUPERNI.4.Data release: releases the 52K instruction dataset and code, enabling the broader community to fine-tune open-source models (leading directly to Alpaca, Vicuna, etc.).
